Details

07
Cancellation rate
Rates were high (24%) in FY23 due to state subsidy disruptions. Under PM Surya Ghar's Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT) system — which replaced unpredictable state-level subsidies — rates have stabilised to ~12–15%. FY26 saw a temporary spike to ~18% due to PSU loan order dynamics, now resolved.
Details
PeriodRatePrimary driver
FY23~24%MP subsidy portal shutdown (5 months); commodity price spike
FY24 H1 (cohort data)10–19%DBT transition; TAT compression underway
FY25 onwards (guided)~15% steady stateDBT era; TAT under 45 days; structurally lower risk
FY26 Q2–Q3~18% (elevated)PSU loan order dynamics; temporary, now normalised

The structural improvement is driven by PM Surya Ghar's DBT model: under the old state-subsidy regime, customers booked solar in anticipation of a subsidy that could be delayed for months or suspended entirely (as happened in MP for 5 months in FY23), creating a large window for cancellations. Under DBT, the subsidy is paid directly to the customer's bank account within 30 days of installation — removing the uncertainty. Additionally, booking-to-install TAT has compressed from 75–90 days to under 45 days, further reducing the cancellation window.

Expansion
10
What percentage of growth comes from market growth vs. share gains?
SolarSquare's growth is powered by two tailwinds: a large underpenetrated market (2.1 million installations completed vs. a 10 million scheme target, against a 50 million total TAM) and active share gains within their cities — from under 6% to 7–8% in one year, with 7 cities already above 20%, driven by a premium product converting at higher rates than competitors.
Details
Tailwind 1 — Market expansion

India has 50 million eligible homes for rooftop solar today, expanding to 70 million by FY35 as new construction and rising electricity consumption bring more households into viability. The PM Surya Ghar scheme targeted 10 million of these (20% of current TAM) with a ₹75,201 Cr (~$8.4 Bn) outlay — as of January 2026, only 2.1 million installations have been completed (21% of scheme target), with just 22% of funds disbursed. Roughly 7.9 million homes remain under the scheme alone, before the 40 million homes outside it. The scheme's bigger impact on SolarSquare has been structural: normalising solar as a purchase decision, driving financing costs from 15–17% to 8%, and building national permit infrastructure that makes the category easier to sell.

Tailwind 2 — In-market share gains

Within their 19 operating cities, market share grew from under 6% (FY25) to 7–8% (FY26), targeting 13–14% by FY27 — with 7 cities already above 20%. This is happening at a price premium: SolarSquare's systems cost ₹2.25–2.50L (~$2.5–2.8k) vs. Tata's ₹2.10L (~$2.3k) and the unorganised market's ₹1.8–1.9L (~$2.0–2.1k). Tata has 63% top-of-mind brand recall but only 4–5% market share; SolarSquare has 2% recall but 8% share — the gap is downstream conversion, driven by the GoodZero performance guarantee (unique in market) and the Speed Order process where a site is measured the day before the sales meeting and the consultant arrives with a completed design and quotation.

Tailwind 3 — Replacement market

A large share of India's installed residential solar base was put in by unorganised local installers with no after-sales infrastructure, no performance guarantees, and limited quality controls. SolarSquare's performance audits find 20–30% of market panels already damaged or underperforming. As these systems age and underperform, homeowners increasingly turn to trusted brands for maintenance and replacement — creating an inbound pull that doesn't require SolarSquare to acquire these customers through marketing. The company has launched a Maintenance-as-a-Service product targeting this cohort, with 1,000+ customers onboarded across 4 cities and a target of 100k maintenance customers by FY27.

11
What happens if the subsidies disappear?
The company already expects PM Surya Ghar to wind down post-FY28 and has modelled a short-term revenue dip. Longer term, it is seen as a positive: market consolidation favours organised players, and SolarSquare is the only company past 10,000 installations. From a customer standpoint, payback extends only modestly. And the broader TAM — 40 million homes still to go solar — is independent of the subsidy.
Details
Market structure impact — the primary story

SolarSquare already expects PM Surya Ghar to wind down post-FY28 and has modelled a revenue dip in the transition year. Beyond that short-term dip, subsidy removal is net positive for SolarSquare. The scheme brought in a large number of fly-by-night installers: of ~20,000 installers registered on the National Solar Portal, only 30 have completed more than 1,000 installations and SolarSquare is the only one past 10,000. When the subsidy pulse fades, unorganised players exit and market share consolidates around brands with financing relationships, after-sales infrastructure, and performance guarantees. The FY23 MP subsidy shutdown is the precedent: cancellations spiked to ~24% that year but fully recovered as customers with genuine conviction remained.

Consumer payback impact — secondary, and manageable

Per SolarSquare's Jan-26 approach document modelling a standard 3.24 kW system, payback extends from 3.6 to 4.6 years post-subsidy, partially offset by panel price declines from ₹21–22/watt (~$0.23–0.24/watt) to ₹17/watt (~$0.19/watt) as manufacturing scales.

The bigger picture — TAM independent of subsidy

The subsidy addresses a fraction of the overall opportunity. India has 50 million eligible homes of which only ~2.5–3 million have gone solar — roughly 5–6% penetration. PM Surya Ghar's entire 10 million target, if fully achieved, would bring penetration to ~20%. The remaining 40 million homes represent a market solar must win on its own economics — and with a 4–5 year payback, the fundamentals are there independent of any subsidy.

12
Cohort-level referrals by city
Company-wide long-term average: 30–35% of acquisitions via referrals. Solar installations are naturally viral — visible panels, neighbourhood word of mouth, and social sharing drive organic leads. The SolarPro program converts high-volume referrers into a paid semi-professional channel.
Details

The long-term company-wide average has been 30–35% of acquisitions via referrals. Solar panels have a naturally viral dynamic: installations are visible on rooftops, customers post on social media, and neighbours ask questions — creating organic lead generation that other consumer categories don't have. The Seed memo noted 30% of customers gave a referral lead within 1 week of installation.

The SolarPro program converts high-volume referrers into a paid semi-professional channel, creating a community of engaged brand advocates. City or cohort-level referral breakdowns are not available.
